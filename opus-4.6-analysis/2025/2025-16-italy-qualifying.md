# 2025 Round 16 -- Italian Grand Prix -- Qualifying
**Circuit:** Autodromo Nazionale Monza (5.793 km, 11 corners, 2 DRS zones) | **Date:** Saturday, September 6, 2025

---

## Executive Summary

Max Verstappen delivered the fastest qualifying lap in the history of Formula One, setting a time of 1:18.792 at an average speed of 264.681 km/h (164.465 mph) to claim pole position at Monza. The lap, set on the final run in Q3 with Red Bull's extreme low-downforce rear wing, was the product of a bold setup call that Verstappen himself pushed through against the initial reservations of his engineering team. Red Bull had switched to an even skinnier rear wing flap during FP3 -- one that traded corner stability for minimal drag -- and Verstappen's conviction that it was the right direction proved decisive.

Lando Norris came desperately close, qualifying second just 0.077 seconds behind. The McLaren driver had benefited from a small tow off teammate Oscar Piastri on his final flying lap and set a 1:18.869 that briefly looked like pole before Verstappen's final effort trumped it. Piastri himself was third, 0.190 seconds off, confirming the MCL39's strength at low-downforce venues but not quite enough to overhaul the Red Bull's remarkable straight-line efficiency. Charles Leclerc was a solid fourth for Ferrari at their home race, 0.215 seconds adrift, while Lewis Hamilton completed the top five at +0.332 seconds, though a five-place grid penalty from Zandvoort meant he would start the race from tenth.

The slipstream was, as ever, king at Monza. Tsunoda was deployed early in Q3 to give Verstappen a tow on his first run, at the expense of his own qualifying position -- the Red Bull driver finishing a distant tenth in Q3 having been denied a slipstream on his final attempt. At the other end of the field, Gasly and Hadjar both qualified knowing they would start from the pit lane after exceeding power unit component allocations, making their Q1 eliminations less consequential but highlighting the strategic engine penalty decisions teams were making at the Temple of Speed.

## Top 3 Car Technical Analysis

### P1 -- Red Bull RB21 (Verstappen) -- 1:18.792

The RB21 was the weekend's revelation. Red Bull arrived at Monza with a new floor -- likely their final significant upgrade before pivoting to 2026 development -- and deployed their extreme low-downforce rear wing that had previously been used at Silverstone. During FP3, the crew made a last-minute switch to an even more aggressive configuration, featuring a dramatically reduced upper flap with a minimised chord to slash drag to its absolute minimum. The trade-off was visible: the car's rear was visibly loose through Parabolica and the Lesmo corners throughout practice.

The Honda RBPTH003 power unit was operating at its peak, and the RB21's natural aerodynamic efficiency -- its ability to generate a competitive level of downforce with less drag penalty than rivals -- was perfectly suited to Monza's demands. In the speed trap, the Red Bull was not the fastest (the Saubers were over 3 km/h quicker through the trap), but the overall package of straight-line speed combined with the minimum downforce needed to brake confidently into the first chicane (Rettifilo) and Variante della Roggia was superior. Verstappen's ability to carry speed through Curva Grande with less rear wing than anyone else on the grid was the defining image of the weekend.

### P2 -- McLaren MCL39 (Norris) -- 1:18.869

The MCL39 confirmed its status as the grid's most aerodynamically efficient car but could not quite match the Red Bull's extreme low-drag configuration at Monza. McLaren ran their standard Monza-spec rear wing, which while aggressive, retained slightly more chord than Red Bull's extreme option. Norris's 1:18.869 was set with a small tow from Piastri, and the McLaren's DRS effectiveness was outstanding -- sector one (containing the main straight) was competitive, with the Mercedes PU showing excellent deployment. However, through the speed trap itself, the McLaren trailed the Saubers and was comparable to the Red Bull. The MCL39's strength was in the braking zones -- Norris and Piastri were particularly strong on the anchors into the Rettifilo chicane, where the car's front-end stability allowed later braking. The Q3 session ultimately came down to Verstappen's bravery through the fast corners versus McLaren's braking precision.

### P3 -- McLaren MCL39 (Piastri) -- 1:18.982

Piastri's lap was clean and composed but lacked the final aggression of both Verstappen's and Norris's efforts. The championship leader appeared to prioritise a safe bank lap rather than pushing to the absolute limit on his final Q3 run. The MCL39's balance was neutral through the Lesmo complex and stable under braking, but Piastri lost time relative to Norris through Curva Grande, where his minimum speed was marginally lower. As the championship leader with a 31-point advantage, a front-row lockout was not essential, and third represented a solid platform for the race.

## Key Observations

1. **Verstappen's FP3 gamble was the story of the weekend.** The decision to switch to an even more extreme low-downforce rear wing during FP3, against the initial concerns of his engineering team about race-day tyre degradation, was a masterclass in driver-team collaboration. Verstappen's conviction that the skinnier wing was the right call -- and his ability to drive around its rear instability -- was the decisive factor in the qualifying result. His radio message "It worked!" after pole was telling.

2. **Speed trap data revealed surprising hierarchies.** The Saubers were the fastest cars through the speed trap by over 3 km/h, confirming their extreme low-drag philosophy. However, this did not translate to overall lap time due to their weaker cornering performance. Among the frontrunners, Verstappen had a slight edge over the McLarens through the trap, which was crucial for maintaining track position. Fernando Alonso topped FP1 speed traps at 349.2 km/h.

3. **The tow was worth approximately 0.2-0.3 seconds.** Tsunoda's Q3 performance without a slipstream (P10, +0.727s) versus his Q2 time with traffic assistance illustrated the enormous value of the tow at Monza. Norris's use of Piastri's slipstream on his final Q3 run was textbook, yet it still was not enough to beat Verstappen's raw pace.

4. **Ferrari's home qualifying was quietly disappointing.** Leclerc's fourth, 0.215 seconds off pole, exposed the SF-25's straight-line deficit at the circuit where it matters most. The tow controversy -- Hamilton's refusal to sacrifice his own lap to provide Leclerc a slipstream -- added a political dimension to Ferrari's home weekend. Hamilton argued he had never provided teammates with tows at McLaren or Mercedes either.

5. **Engine penalty season at Monza.** Gasly (Alpine) and Hadjar (Racing Bulls) both took engine penalties, a traditional Monza strategy where teams accept the pit lane start at a circuit where overtaking is possible. This effectively removed two cars from the genuine qualifying battle.

6. **Red Bull's new floor was a quiet game-changer.** The floor introduced at Monza, described as likely Red Bull's final significant upgrade before focusing on 2026, appeared to improve the RB21's aerodynamic balance enough to unlock the extreme low-downforce wing configuration. The combination of new floor + extreme wing + Verstappen's setup conviction created a car that was genuinely the fastest at a circuit where Red Bull had not been expected to dominate.

7. **Williams's Monza disappointment.** Both Sainz and Albon were eliminated in Q2 at a circuit where the Mercedes-powered Williams should theoretically shine. The FW47's aerodynamic efficiency appears to lag behind the competition even at minimum downforce levels, negating the power unit advantage.
